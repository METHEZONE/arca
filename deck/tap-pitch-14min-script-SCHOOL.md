## SLIDE 1 — Problem, in one scene (≈1:05)
*Visual: dim, quiet. Notification chime on "lights up" (same chime returns on Slide 10).*

> Let me start with the best ninety minutes of my week.
>
> It's late. Everyone's gone home, the messages have stopped, and for the first time all day it's quiet enough to actually think. I'm finally working on what matters — the strategy, the product — and the ideas start connecting faster than I can write them down. This is flow.
>
> And then my phone lights up. A client. *"Send me the business plan — we're deciding right now."* So I stop, dig through folders, and send it. Five minutes later — *"Send the quote and we'll wire the payment today."* No quote, no money. So I build it from scratch. Again. And by the time I look up… the ninety minutes are gone.
>
> Here's the thing — none of that work was hard. Any of it could have been done by someone else. I just didn't have anyone I trusted to do it right. That is the problem our project sets out to solve.

## SLIDE 2 — Field research (≈1:35)
*Visual: interview cohort photos; the 57 / 43 and 68% figures animate in.*

> So this semester, we studied that problem properly. We ran qualitative interviews across four groups — startup founders, office workers, university students, and the venture and incubation experts at Hyundai's ZER01NE — more than forty people in total, using semi-structured interviews and live reactions to an early prototype.
>
> The findings were strikingly consistent. The thing stealing people's time wasn't the work itself — it was everything wrapped around it. Sending documents. Writing quotes. Answering "where are we on this." Low-value, high-urgency, and all of it routed through a single person.
>
> The numbers back this up. Microsoft's Work Trend Index reports that employees spend fifty-seven percent of their time communicating, and only forty-three percent creating. Sixty-eight percent say they don't have enough uninterrupted focus time. And nearly two in three — sixty-four percent — say they struggle to find the time and energy to do their actual job.
>
> And in our own interviews, two sentences kept recurring. One founder told us: *"If I step away for a day, the money stops moving."* Another: *"My real work doesn't start until the requests die down at night."*

## SLIDE 3 — Problem statement + reframe (≈1:10)
*Visual: "The bottleneck is a person." → Save · Summarize · **Handoff**.*

> These point to one root cause. The founder has become the bottleneck. Every payment, every deal, every next step has to pass through them first — not because the tasks are difficult, but because there's no trusted way to hand them off.
>
> And this reframes the whole problem. Most people treat it as an organization problem — they need better notes, better reminders, a smarter to-do list. So that's what existing tools give them: they record the meeting, summarize it, and stack up reminders. But a summary never finishes the work, and a reminder never sends the quote.
>
> The problem was never capturing information. It's delegation — giving a task away completely, so it gets done without you. That's a different category. Not second-brain, which stores more. Not second-self, which clones you. We call it the handoff category — and that's the space ARCA occupies.

## SLIDE 4 — Solution · THE ZONE, ARCA, the verb (≈1:25)
*Visual: THE ZONE worldview; ARCA = arc + archive + ark; slogan.*

> Our solution is built on one idea, which we call THE ZONE. The zone is that state of deep focus — those ninety minutes — where your most valuable work happens. Its single enemy is interruption. And nearly every tool today is designed to *add* interruptions: more notifications, more to check. We designed the opposite — something whose only job is to keep you in the zone.
>
> That something is ARCA — a companion that handles work on your behalf. The name encodes its three functions: *arc,* to connect; *archive,* to remember; *ark,* to protect. Connect, remember, protect.
>
> And it runs on one action: *arca it.* When a request lands, you don't carry it, file it, or add it to a list. You arca it — you hand it over completely, and ARCA executes it. Don't carry it. Just arca it. The crucial part is that this delegation never costs you your focus — which is how it actually works.

## SLIDE 5 — How it works · two modes + pipeline (≈2:10)
*Visual: split "IN THE ZONE (shield)" / "OUT OF THE ZONE (triage queue)"; then a 4-step pipeline strip.*

> ARCA operates in two modes, mirroring how people actually work.
>
> The first mode is when you're in the zone. ARCA stands guard. Requests still arrive — the client still needs the quote — but they don't reach you. ARCA intercepts each one, understands it, and handles everything it already can, silently. You're never asked to approve or decide, because you're never interrupted. Your focus stays whole.
>
> The second mode is when you step out of deep work. ARCA presents everything that arrived while you were away, organized and ready. For routine requests, you arca it — and ARCA drafts the reply, attaches the document, sends it, and closes the loop. For low-risk actions, it simply proceeds without asking. And for anything needing real judgment, it offers two or three options with a recommendation, and you decide with a single tap.
>
> Underneath both modes runs a four-step pipeline. First, capture: ARCA ingests any input — a meeting, a voice memo, a message — and produces a speaker-separated transcript using ElevenLabs' Scribe model, so it knows exactly who asked for what. Second, it builds what we call an Action Pack — not a summary, but an executable bundle: the extracted to-dos, a drafted reply, a calendar entry, ready to go. Third, it executes, pushing results into the tools you already use, like Slack, Notion, and your document storage. And fourth, it reflects — a weekly recap that shows not just what you did, but the causes behind it: which decisions led where, and what kept pulling you out of focus.
>
> So ARCA doesn't just record your work. It completes it — and protects the conditions that let you do your best work in the first place.

## SLIDE 6 — Prototype demonstration (≈1:50)
*Visual: full-screen screen-recording of the working app.*

> ARCA is a working prototype, so let me demonstrate it with the quote scenario from earlier.
>
> A request arrives — *"send me the quote."* I pass it to ARCA. Notice it doesn't simply transcribe the message — it interprets it. It identifies who's asking, what they need, and that a payment depends on the response.
>
> It then assembles the reply — the correct document, the right figures — ready to send. Now the core action: I select *arca it.* The response goes directly to the client. The deal moves forward, and I never opened a folder or left what I was doing. My only input was a single tap.
>
> And for a request that needs judgment — here ARCA presents two options with a recommendation. I choose one, and the loop is closed.
>
> This is the entire value proposition in one interaction: a task that would normally fragment an hour of focus, resolved in seconds, without pulling the user out of the zone. And every layer — transcription, analysis, action planning, integration — is functional today, not a mockup.

## SLIDE 7 — User validation (≈1:15)
*Visual: prototype-in-hands photos; pull-quotes from the cohorts.*

> Before finalizing the design, we put that prototype in front of the same groups — founders, office workers, and the ZER01NE experts — and let them react to it live.
>
> Two things came back clearly. First, they validated the core flow. The single feature they most wanted was exactly this — automatically turning a conversation into a ready-to-go bundle of actions. And the triage design — two or three options, with low-risk items handled automatically — matched what they said they actually wanted.
>
> Second, their sharpest feedback was that our original vision was too broad. So we narrowed it, to one definition we now lead with: *the AI that remembers your conversations and turns them into your next action.* The experts also confirmed our central thesis — that the moat isn't the transcription technology, which is now a commodity, but the productivity impact and the memory that accumulates over time.

## SLIDE 8 — Novelty of the solution (≈1:45)
*Visual: positioning matrix (surface vs complete / interrupt vs protect); three pillars.*

> Now, the novelty of our solution — because transcription and AI summaries already exist, and the technology itself is not what makes ARCA different.
>
> Tools like Granola, Otter, and CLOVA Note capture meetings well — Granola alone was recently valued at one-and-a-half billion dollars — but they stop at capture. They surface information and add to your notifications. In our framing, they aren't really an alternative; they pull you further out of the zone. And the AI agents that *do* act — like Lindy or Copilot — are locked inside their own app or suite; they can't reach across the tools you already use. The honest alternative to ARCA was never a note-taking app. It was hiring a person you trust — an assistant, a chief of staff — which is expensive, slow, and requires training.
>
> ARCA's novelty is delivering that trusted delegation as software, and it rests on three things. First — completion: we measure one metric, how many loops were actually closed, not how good the notes were. Second — behavior: by reducing delegation to a single action, "arca it," we aim to make it a habit, the way "google it" became one — and a habit is far harder to displace than a feature. Third — our roadmap: a marketplace where users build and share their own delegation agents, a network effect that a better model alone cannot replicate.

## SLIDE 9 — Feasibility, market & model (≈1:20)
*Visual: working-prototype screenshot; $10–30/mo · 67–85% margin · $3.5B → $21.5B; privacy badges.*

> Finally, feasibility. ARCA isn't a concept — the prototype you just saw runs today. Its costs are low and known: transcription through ElevenLabs' Scribe, at about twenty-two cents an hour, paired with Claude for the analysis. That supports a simple subscription — ten to thirty dollars a month — with gross margins between sixty-seven and eighty-five percent.
>
> And because recording conversations demands trust, privacy is built into the design, not bolted on: clear consent, a defined retention policy, and an on-device processing option.
>
> The market is large and growing — AI assistants for knowledge work are projected to expand from roughly three-and-a-half billion dollars to over twenty-one billion within five years. By occupying the handoff category, rather than the crowded note-taking space, we position ourselves early in that growth.

*Visual: return to Slide 1's scene; slogan lands full screen; same chime.*

> So, to conclude — return to where we began. It's late, everyone's gone home, and you're finally in the zone. Your phone lights up: *"send me the quote."* But this time, you don't stop, you don't dig through folders, and you don't lose those ninety minutes. You simply… arca it.
>
> That is ARCA — a flow-state guardian that completes your work, so you can stay in yours. Don't carry it. Just arca it. Thank you.
